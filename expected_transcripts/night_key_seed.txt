[lobby_arrival]
The lobby smells like wet wool and old lemon polish. Rain needles the glass. You sign in for the night shift and the desk phone blinks once—then goes still. Upstairs, a guest has not been seen since dusk.
> Walk the hallway and listen at doors.
[hallway_listen]
The hallway carpet drinks your footsteps. Behind one door, a TV murmurs. Behind another—nothing. The quiet has weight.
> Try the service stairwell.
[service_stairs_locked]
The service door is locked. A small sign reads STAFF ONLY. The keyhole looks recently scratched.
> Go find maintenance.
[find_maintenance]
In the back corridor, a maintenance worker wipes his hands on a rag. He watches you the way people watch trouble approaching.
> Ask for the master key.
[ask_key]
“We don’t just hand that out,” he says. Then, quieter: “Not tonight.”
> Press him.
[press_key]
He studies your face. “If I give you the key,” he says, “you’re in it with me.”
> Promise you won’t blame him.
[key_promise]
He nods once and slips the master key into your palm like a secret.
> Return to the hallway.
[hallway_listen]
The hallway carpet drinks your footsteps. Behind one door, a TV murmurs. Behind another—nothing. The quiet has weight.
> Try the service stairwell door with the master key.
[service_stairs_open]
The master key turns too smoothly, as if it has been used tonight already. The service stairwell smells like damp concrete and old detergent. One flight down, a second door waits with a deadbolt and a smear of fresh paint.
> Go down one flight.
[basement_landing]
The stairwell closes behind you with a soft click. The air is colder here, and the concrete holds the day’s damp like a memory. You count what’s left of you—4—and wish the number meant something more than “keep moving.”
Halfway down, a second door waits. Someone has rolled fresh paint across it like a hurried apology.
> Pause and breathe warmth into your hands.
[basement_warmth]
You press your palms together and breathe. The small act feels ridiculous, but it gives you back something you didn’t know you were losing.
Warmth: 4.
> Try the painted door now.
[painted_door]
Up close, the paint is still tacky. It smells like a cover story. The knob is worn smooth from hands that pretended this was routine.
> Use the master key.
[room_404]
The room beyond is not a guest room. It is a place where the building keeps what it doesn’t want seen: spare linens, a cracked mirror, and a chair that has been dragged to the center as if someone waited there for hours.
On the chair is a folded message, weighed down with a tarnished room key.
You flip to the page in your notebook where you list what you’ve confirmed tonight: found_master_key, room_404_opened, trusted_maintenance.
> Read the folded message.
[read_message]
The handwriting is careful, almost polite.
“If you find this, I did not vanish. I left because the hotel makes vanishing convenient. Room 404 is not a room. It is where they put the story when they need it to stop.”
The note ends with a time, a name you don’t recognize, and one sentence that lands like a weight: “Please don’t let them call it nothing.”
> Tell the manager what you found (only if you can keep your voice kind).
[confront_manager]
The manager is in his office when you find him, coat still on, as if he never intends to stay long enough to be responsible for the night.
You place the tarnished key on his desk. You do not raise your voice. You do not give him the drama he expects.
For a moment he looks relieved. Then he looks angry—because relief means you could have chosen otherwise.
> Insist on doing the right thing anyway.
[call_police_now]
You pick up the lobby phone and dial anyway.
The dispatcher asks for details. You give them what you have: the missing guest, the painted door, the message, the key that shouldn’t exist. Outside, the rain keeps moving, indifferent as always, but inside the lobby something changes.
You are not solving everything tonight. You are refusing the convenient story. You will stay until they arrive, even if you have only 3 left to give.
Whatever this place is, it does not get to make people disappear into “nothing.”
You hang up and wait.
End.
